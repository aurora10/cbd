Understanding Cannabinoids: How Is CBN Produced?

Cannabinoids are molecular compounds produced by the cannabis plant. Cannabis creates these compounds to serve several purposes, including warding away insect predators and grazing animals, protecting the plant from harsh weather, and attracting pollinators. There are around 113 cannabinoids known in the cannabis plant, with the two most famous cannabinoids being cannabidiol, or CBD, and tetrahydrocannabinol, or THC. 

CBD and THC are also the two cannabinoids that appear in the greatest quantities in cannabis, which is why they are referred to as the “major cannabinoids.” CBD is non-psychoactive (it doesn’t cause a high) and carries with it a number of health benefits, some of which we’ll discuss in a moment. Delta-9 THC is psychoactive and does cause a high … hence all the laws and regulations. Here at CBDfx, we use the low-THC form of cannabis, known as hemp, to create all of our legal CBD and delta-9 THC products. 

But there are other cannabinoids, as well, which occur in lesser quantities and are known as “minor cannabinoids.” These include CBG, CBC, and the star of this article, CBN (cannabinol). 

What Do Cannabinoids Do?

Cannabinoids interact with our endocannabinoid system, which is a series of chemical receptors closely integrated with our central and peripheral nervous systems, as well as systems that regulate digestion, sleep, and more. Humans and all other vertebrate animals have an endocannabinoid system, including our dogs and cats.

When a cannabinoid, like CBD or CBN, interacts with an endocannabinoid system receptor, it imparts specific therapeutic benefits, depending on what that specific receptor does and what that cannabinoid’s properties are. 

Even though the history of humans using cannabis stretches back for millennia, and scientists have been studying it for more than a century, much of what happens when cannabinoids interact with our bodies is still a mystery. While scientists continue to study the effects of multiple cannabinoids for uses including epilepsy, pain relief, and anxiety, the research is ongoing.

What Is CBN?

It’s interesting to note that, even though CBN is far less known than CBD, it’s actually the very first cannabinoid ever to have been isolated in the cannabis plant. CBN was first isolated in the late 1800s. By the 1930s, scientist R.S. Cahn was studying CBN in depth, and soon scientists were synthesizing this cannabinoid.

CBN is short for cannabinol. CBN is actually formed by the breakdown of THC over time, often because of oxidation or heat. In fact, CBN was probably first identified in the 1800s because of poor storage of cannabis during transportation.

But CBN is hardly a weaker version of THC, as one might think. While the powerful psychoactive features of THC are long gone by the time CBN comes onto the scene, there are a multitude of great health benefits that take their place.

The Benefits of CBN

As we mentioned, the research on CBN, CBD and other cannabinoids is ongoing and the FDA wisely cautions against making definitive medical claims. That said, we know what people use CBN for, and how cannabinol works for them.

For a long time, people have depended on the calming effects of CBN to help them get a good night’s rest. CBN binds with receptors in the brain, and seems to promote calm and sleepiness. In fact, many people buy CBN products strictly for sleep support. Often, CBN is combined with CBD and other calm-promoting ingredients, such as melatonin and certain terpenes, for products designed to enhance sleep, as well as promote calmness and ease stress.

CBN works most powerfully in combination with other cannabinoids to produce the Entourage Effect. This is the effect created when multiple cannabinoids (such as CBD and CBN) work synergistically, creating an overall effect stronger than what any single cannabinoid would produce on its own.

Mushrooms as Medicine: A Brief History

We’ve used mushrooms as medicine since Neolithic times, going as far back as 10,000 BCE. In fact, the 4,000-year-old mummy Ötzi, or the Iceman, was found to have an antibiotic mushroom in his medical kit.

In 450 BCE, the Greek physician Hippocrates (namesake of the Hippocratic Oath that doctors take today), used the amadou mushroom as an anti-inflammatory. Indigenous peoples in North America used puffball mushrooms to heal wounds, and the 5th century Chinese alchemist Tao Hongjing wrote about the power of multiple types of mushroom.

In every culture across the globe, mushrooms and fungi have been a treasured source of health and medicine for thousands of years.

But, before you begin to think of mushroom medicine as just the stuff of history and folklore, it’s valuable to remember that many important medicines we use today are derived from fungi. 

This includes penicillin (from the fungus penicillium notatum), the anti-cancer therapeutic Krestin (tramates versicolor, or turkey tail, which we’ll talk about more below), and the drug Cyclosporine, used to prevent organ rejection after transplants (two fungi, trichoderms polysporum and cylindrocarpon lucidum). And those are just a few examples of the medical power of mushrooms.

Western science, unfortunately, often overlooks indigenous and traditional medicines. Fortunately, an overwhelming number of studies into mushrooms show that the polysaccharides and triterpenes in mushrooms illustrate their immense medical and adaptogenic properties — something that our ancestors already knew. 

For generation upon generation, we have relied on mushrooms and fungi for hundreds of uses, including fighting stress, infection, inflammation, and fatigue — as well as providing vitamin content in our diet. 

Adaptogenic Mushrooms for Health & Wellness

When we talk about using mushrooms for wellness applications, we need to understand the difference between medicinal mushrooms and adaptogenic mushrooms. Medicinal mushrooms possess properties that have specific medicinal applications, like anti-inflammatory or antibacterial use. 

While some adaptogenic mushrooms do double duty as medicinal mushrooms, the term “adaptogenic” refers to another set of incredibly valuable properties.

Adaptogens have the potential to dramatically reduce the effects of stress on our bodies — physical, chemical, and biological stress.

The term comes from the Latin word adaptare, meaning to adapt or adjust. It was coined by the Russian scientist Nikolai Lazarev in the 1940s when he came across the potential of adaptogenic substances to reduce stress on the bodies of athletes.

Confirming what the global cultures we mentioned above have known for millennia, much adaptogenic research since Lazarev has focused on the benefits of specific mushrooms, including chaga, cordyceps, lion’s mane, maitake, reishi, shiitake, and turkey tail mushroom. 

How Do Adaptogenic Mushrooms Work?

Adaptogens interact with the body’s primary stress response system, the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis (HPA axis). This is the interaction between the hypothalamus, pituitary gland, and adrenal glands. 

The HPA axis, much like the endocannabinoid system, also helps regulate processes like digestion, mood, and immune response. It also influences the release of cortisol.

Adaptogens influence the HPA axis when it is stimulated by stress, helping our bodies return to homeostasis, or balance. 

The polysaccharides and triterpenes in mushrooms do a lot of the heavy lifting when it comes to adaptogenic work. Polysaccharides, like beta glucan, have been shown to enhance immune response, and triterpenes can help with mood in addition to supporting biological responses, like blood pressure and perhaps even pain response.

Triterpenes may sound familiar to you CBD fans. That’s because they’re a type of compound called a terpenoid that is closely related to the terpenes we look for in CBD for the Entourage Effect. Triterpenes and terpenes are a little different from one another (terpenes are hydrocarbons, while terpenoids come about through the oxidation of terpenes), but they are just as valuable in their adaptogenic potential, while medicinal mushrooms have been used for overall wellness and to treat all kinds of ailments for hundreds of years, it needs to be stated that most of the studies that have been conducted were very small human studies or in animals. These fungi have huge potential health benefits, but more research is definitely needed.

Adaptogenic Mushrooms: The Star Players

There are around 14,000 known species of mushrooms, and that’s considered to be only 10% of the mushrooms that are estimated to exist here on Earth. Some have medicinal properties, some are poisonous, and some make for a nice salad recipe when they’re dried out. So, needless to say, we need to do some narrowing down.

Let’s look at the most popular mushrooms with adaptogenic properties. You could say any one of these is the fungi you want to bring to the party (sorry, we couldn’t resist).

Cordyceps

Cordyceps sinensis is a type of fungus known for its potential to reduce stress on the body, in part by increasing our resistance to stress-inducing activity. Cordyceps is often used for an energy boost. 

Chaga

Inonotus obliquus gets its nickname, chaga, from a medieval Russian prince who purportedly took it to treat his lip (gaga) cancer … or so the story goes. We certainly don’t make any claims about the use of chaga for any serious disease, but we can tell you that the chaga mushroom has been relied upon for centuries to help with immunity and overall health.

Lion’s Mane

The hericium erinaceus mushroom gets its nickname from its shaggy appearance. This mushroom has also traditionally been taken for its adaptogenic benefits, helping the body to regulate stress and inflammation.

Reishi

Ganoderma lucidum mushrooms are believed to help support the adrenal glands, which in turn release cortisol to help the body respond to stress. In China, this mushroom is known as ling zhi, which translates roughly to “spiritual plant.”

Maitake

The grifola frondosa mushroom has been called the “dancing mushroom” (maitake) because it was so valuable in feudal Japan that people danced for joy when they found some. It’s nutrient-rich and beloved for its positive immunity and wellness benefits. 

Turkey tail 

The trametes versicolor mushroom gets the name “turkey tail” (also “turkeytail”) from its colorful, fan-like shape. This mushroom is well known for its immune properties, and it contains two different polysaccharopeptides, which have powerful immune benefits. It’s also packed with antioxidants and other beneficial compounds.

Shiitake

Lentinula edodes is a brown-capped mushroom that hails from East Asia and is chock full of essential vitamins and minerals. Shiitake has long been valued for its immunity, heart health, antibacterial, adaptogenic, and nootropic benefits — all of which makes it a wonderful, well-rounded wellness supplement.

Psilocybin and Magic Mushrooms

Slow your roll, Timothy Leary. The adaptogenic mushrooms in our products are not psychedelic “shrooms.” They won’t make you hallucinate, and they won’t get you high.

They’re not “magic mushrooms.” None of the fungi in our CBD mushroom products contain psilocybin, the hallucinogenic compound that people use for recreational purposes. 

Our products aren’t designed for intoxicating effects. Rather, we’re interested in wellness benefits — helping mood and overall health, and contributing to a healthy lifestyle.

Mushrooms for the Mind: Nootropics

In addition to their adaptogenic properties, certain mushrooms — like lion’s mane, cordyceps and chaga — have the potential to provide even more value to our holistic health as nootropics.

A nootropic is a substance that is believed to enhance cognitive function. Although we originally used the term for chemical pharmaceuticals designed specifically for a brain boost (Adderall, donepezil), the definition has since expanded to include natural supplements — including mushrooms.

 How could a nootropic help with brain function? There are a few ways: by stimulating the repair and growth of new nervous system cells, fighting brain cell inflammation, maximizing oxygen use in brain and nervous tissue, and supporting mood and sleep, which is absolutely vital for brain health. 

There’s great potential in the use of mushrooms for their nootropic benefits alone, much less when supported by their adaptogenic and medicinal properties.

CBD BBQ Sauce

Great for wings, ribs, or anything you may be grilling, this Memphis-style sauce is a great complement to dry rubs, and works as a dipping sauce or to brush onto wings or ribs. 

Makes 12 servings

 

Ingredients

12 droppers CBDfx CBD oil

1 tbsp vegetable oil

29 oz tomato sauce

1/2 yellow onion, diced

2 cloves garlic, minced

3/4 cup brown sugar 

1/3 cup apple cider vinegar

3 tbsp molasses

2 tbsp Worcestershire sauce

2 tbsp yellow mustard

1 tbsp chili powder

1/2 tsp cayenne pepper

1 tsp kosher salt

1 tsp coarse ground black pepper

 

Directions

  1. Add vegetable oil and diced onion to a large pot on medium heat
  2. Cook your onions until they’re just starting to brown and are smelling amazing (about 6 minutes)
  3. Stir in the garlic
  4. Add sugar, tomato sauce, vinegar, molasses, Worcestershire sauce and your remaining dry ingredients and stir it all up.
  5. Cook uncovered for 10 minutes
  6. Add CBDfx CBD oil
  7. Cook until thickened — 10–15 minutes

What Is CBD?

CBD is the common abbreviation for cannabidiol. You have to admit, “cannabidiol” doesn’t sound like the sexiest cocktail ingredient ever. 

CBD is one of many therapeutic molecular compounds called cannabinoids found in the cannabis plant. Unlike its famous but distant relative, THC, CBD is not psychoactive. That means it won’t get you high.

This is a very important distinction when it comes to mixing a cocktail.

While CBD does not get you high, it does impart a host of benefits that people describe as calming, relaxing, and causing a distinct “chill” feeling.

How Does CBD Work?

Sure, you’ve heard people talk themselves blue over the chill benefits of CBD. But what’s the science here?

CBD and its cousin cannabinoids, like CBG and CBN, interact with receptors in what’s known as the endocannabinoid system. This is a system that exists in all mammals, including our dogs and cats, and you and me.

The endocannabinoid system is integrated with our central and peripheral nervous systems, as well as systems that regulate sleep, digestion, and immunity. When a cannabinoid interacts with a receptor in this system, it passes along the therapeutic benefits we have come to love it for.

Are CBD Cocktails Safe?

If CBD comes from the cannabis plant, and I’m mixing it with alcohol, is a CBD cocktail safe?

Short answer: yes. 

Since CBD isn’t psychoactive, we don’t have to worry about adding a high on top of the intoxicating effects of alcohol. 

In addition, some studies imply that CBD might reduce blood alcohol concentration and ward against alcohol-induced damage to our cells. While we find that exciting, it’s crucial to note that the FDA doesn’t support any definitive claims about this.

Rather than compounding the intoxicating effects of alcohol, CBD works its own magic, providing the relaxing benefits people seek it out for.

The key to an enjoyable CBD cocktail experience is in taste and dosage. As we’ll mention below, you’ll need to get familiar with how your CBD tincture tastes, so you know how it will contribute to the overall taste of your drink. Similarly, know how much CBD oil works best for you. Don’t make CBD cocktails with a heavy hand. Use just the amount you would normally imbibe.

As with anything involving alcohol, moderation and ethical use are absolutely critical. Since CBD and alcohol will work together to some degree, it’s important to moderate how much you consume, as too much will likely make you very sleepy. Also, you should always inform anyone about what’s in their drink before you hand it to them.
