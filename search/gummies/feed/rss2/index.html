What Is CBD Oil?

CBD oil, or hemp oil, is the oil that is derived from the stalks, buds, and leaves of the cannabis hemp plant. This is not to be confused with hemp-seed oil, which doesn’t contain CBD or other cannabinoids. CBD oil, though, contains a rich blend of all the cannabinoids, terpenes, flavonoids, and healthy fatty acids of the cannabis hemp plant.

Full Spectrum CBD

Full-spectrum CBD is the initial extract from the hemp plant with no additional filtration. That means it contains all of the 100-plus cannabinoids of the hemp plant, including CBD, CBG, CBC, and even trace amounts of THC (less than 0.3% by law). This oil is also rich in terpenes (which give the hemp plant its scent and taste) and flavonoids (which give hemp its color). All of these chemical compounds, as well as those healthy fatty acids we mentioned) provide a wealth of health and wellness benefits.

But the effects of these various compounds are more than merely the sum of their individual parts. It turns out that multiple cannabinoids, when taken together, actually bolster one another’s effects through a phenomenon known as the Entourage Effect. So, when you take a product that contains full-spectrum CBD, you not only benefit from all the individual cannabinoids and other compounds, but you also get them to perform at peak power and effectiveness.

Broad spectrum CBD is pretty much the same oil as full spectrum, only a filtration process takes place to remove the THC. This filtration also takes out a few of the other compounds in the process. Nevertheless, most of the cannabinoids, terpenes, and flavonoids remain in the oil, giving you a CBD oil that is still quite robust and can deliver a solid, albeit slightly diminished Entourage Effect.

CBD isolate is CBD with all other cannabis compounds removed, leaving you with a powder that’s 99% pure CBD. CBD isolate does not deliver the Entourage Effect, as it contains no complementary cannabinoids, but it does offer a flavorless, odorless form of CBD that can be useful for products, like vape oils or bath products, where the product designer can make use of a clean, open palette.

A Guide to Hemp https://cbdfx.com/where-does-cbd-come-from-a-guide-to-hemp/ Sat, 19 Nov 2022 00:33:49 +0000 https://cbdfx.com/?p=2098323 It’s a plant that’s been used by humans for thousands of years, for everything from rope and paper manufacturing to pain relief and other wellness benefits. It’s also suffered from a basic misunderstanding due to its unfair association with marijuana. The plant we’re talking about is, of course, hemp. And it’s not only a plant of historical significance; it’s also the source of the hottest health supplement of the past decade: CBD.

What Is Hemp?

Hemp is a form of the cannabis plant. Cannabis has been around for a long, long time. How long? Think dinosaurs-long. That’s right, the cannabaceae family dates back to the Cretaceous time period, with the oldest surviving fossils clocking in at 90 to 94 million years old!

Humans began domesticating these plants as far back as 12,000 years ago in East Asia. Some of cannabis’s early use was, as one might expect, for the mood-lifting effects associated with THC. And it was used for medicinal purposes, as well, including pain relief and sleep support. As a hearty stalk plant, cannabis came to be used to make rope, cloth, paper, and a host of other products. It’s safe to say that cannabis has been an integral part of human civilization, though early 20th century laws in the US and beyond changed things for a number of years.

As the government cracked down on high-THC cannabis, commonly referred to as marijuana, low-THC hemp got caught in the crossfire. It wasn’t until the 2018 US Farm Bill that hemp was finally legalized at the national level.

So, how do we connect the dots from hemp to all those great CBD gummies and other wellness products people have been enjoying in recent years? Well, let’s start by taking a closer look at the hemp plant itself.

What Are Trichomes?

Trichomes are tiny growths on the surface of the cannabis plant. They produce a wide range of compounds that protect the plant. These include cannabinoids (such as CBD and THC), terpenes, and flavonoids.

Terpenes give cannabis its flavor and scent. Flavonoids give plants their color. Like terpenes and flavonoids, the 100-plus cannabinoid compounds also serve a wide variety of functions, including protection against insect predators, grazing animals, UV light, and inclement weather. These compounds also attract pollinators, thus perpetuating the species.

What Are Cannabinoids?

As mentioned, cannabis produces over a hundred cannabinoid compounds. The two most prevalent (or, “major cannabinoids”) are CBD (cannabidiol) and THC (tetrahydrocannabinol). Marijuana is heavy on the THC and a bit lighter in CBD content. Hemp is the opposite: high in CBD and low in THC. In fact, there is so little THC in hemp (less than 0.3%) that it’s non-intoxicating. Minor cannabinoids include CBN (cannabinol), CBG (cannabigerol), and CBC (cannabichromene), along with many others.

Each of these compounds has a unique effect on the endocannabinoid system, found in the bodies of all vertebrate animals. It turns out, we also create cannabinoids. Our internally generated cannabinoids, called endocannabinoids, serve as neurotransmitters, communicating with receptors in various systems and organs throughout the body, helping them to maintain homeostasis, or balance. This, in turn, helps the body to regulate such crucial functions as sleep, pain, stress, motor control, immunity, mood, memory, appetite, and more.

CBD, THC, and other plant-based cannabinoids (phytocannabinoids) interact with these same receptors, when consumed, and help to support these very same functions. The wellness benefits that stem from this interaction are the reason that CBD has become such a popular health supplement.

Extracting CBD

CBD oil is extracted from the stalks, leaves, and buds of the hemp plant. This is different from hemp seed oil which, of course, comes from the seeds. While the oil from hemp seeds is great for skin care, it does not contain the same health-boosting compounds as CBD oil (sometimes called hemp oil). The initial extraction contains all of those compounds, including all cannabinoids, terpenes, flavonoids, and healthy fatty acids of the plant. This extract is called full spectrum CBD.

Because full spectrum CBD contains trace amounts of THC (less than 0.3%, by law), many people prefer to have the THC filtered out. This filtered version is called broad spectrum CBD. Broad spectrum oil is still a robust oil, containing nearly all the other hemp compounds of full spectrum, and is the desired oil for many users.

Some people prefer to have all other hemp material removed, leaving only the CBD. This 99% pure CBD is called CBD isolate. It’s a flavorless, odorless powder that can be useful in products, like some vape and bath products that require a clean palette for the product designer.

The Importance of Organic Farming & Safe Oil Extraction

Hemp is what’s known as a bioaccumulator, meaning that it sucks up pretty much everything from the soil that surrounds the plant. This can be great when it comes to absorbing nutrients. In fact, it’s this powerful absorption that makes it easy to grow hemp (and all cannabis) in a wide variety of climates.

However, when the surrounding soil contains pesticides, herbicides, heavy metals, and other toxins, that can be a problem. Ultimately, all of those potentially harmful compounds will not only be absorbed into the plant, but they’ll also end up in the CBD oil that’s extracted from the plant and, yes, they’ll be right there in the CBD products made from that oil. So, it’s vital to find CBD companies who work with farmers using organic techniques to grow their hemp crops. It’s the only way to ensure the purity of your hemp oil.

It’s also important to know how the CBD oil was extracted from the hemp. Some companies use cheaper methods involving solvents, such as butane, for extraction. This can leave behind potentially harmful residues in the oil. Though it might cost a bit more, it’s worth it to find a company that uses carbon dioxide to remove the CBD from the hemp. CO2 doesn’t leave behind a residue and is actually more efficient in pulling the cannabinoids from the hemp. So, you get a more pure and potent product when you choose a brand that uses CO2 extraction.

You can verify the purity and the potency of your product by looking for a Certificate of Analysis (COA), which is a lab report generated by an independent, third-party laboratory. This report will tell you how much CBD, THC, and other cannabinoids are in your product, as well as any potentially harmful compounds that may have found their way into the mix. Never purchase a hemp product that is not accompanied by a COA.

Final Thoughts

Cannabis has a long and colorful history, stretching all the way back to the days of the T.Rex. Though it was vilified for nearly a century, due to its association with marijuana, low-THC hemp has rebounded to become one of the hottest health supplements of the 21st century.

As you explore hemp products, make sure to keep in mind some of the tips we mentioned in this article. Look for products made from organically grown hemp, with the oil extracted by means of clean CO2, and accompanied by a Certificate of Analysis. This will allow you to find the purest and most potent products available — and ensure your safety and satisfaction!

What Is Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)?

Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) is a mood disorder that occurs for some people at the same time every year. For most people, that time of year is fall and winter, when there’s less daylight and we generally spend less time outdoors. SAD is generally characterized by elevated feelings of depression or anxiety. Warning signs can include a constant state of being sad or cranky, fatigue, concentration problems, greater appetite and weight gain, the desire to be alone, and even suicidal thoughts. 

While research into this phenomenon is ongoing and we’re not certain of the exact causes of SAD, it’s believed that seasonal hormone changes are the biggest culprit. With less sunlight in the fall and winter, the brain makes less serotonin, which helps to regulate mood. When your serotonin levels are off, this can result in feelings of anxiety or depression.

 

There is a less common summer variation of SAD (often called, “summer depression”), as well, which is characterized by loss of appetite, insomnia, and weight loss.
